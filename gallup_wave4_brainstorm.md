## Background

We have Waves 1-3 of a nationally representative Gen Z panel (N=6484) with parent-child dyads. Wave 1 measured AI usage frequency across 9 domains, beliefs about AI effects (smarter, lazier, happier), self-reported impacts (curiosity, laziness, confidence), individual differences (ambition, loneliness, social anxiety), and open-ended responses. Parent-child belief correlations were moderate (r = .045-.343), and parental worry showed minimal association with child usage.

**Key gap in existing data:** We measure *how much* people use AI but not *how* they use it. We can't distinguish strategic users from passive copy-pasters. Wave 4 should fix this.

**Guiding principle:** Every item should feed at least one active paper. We identified three item blocks plus a light experimental manipulation.

---

## Block 1: AI Use Strategies (feeds Coach not Crutch paper)

**Rationale:** The Coach not Crutch paper shows AI can help learning even while reducing effort — but the mechanism is *how* you use it. These items capture strategic vs. passive use, creating a composite that can be linked to W1-3 attitudes longitudinally.

**Proposed items (1-6 Likert):**
1. "When I use AI, I try to solve the problem myself first"
2. "I ask AI to explain its reasoning, not just give answers"
3. "I use AI to check my own work"
4. "I usually copy what AI gives me without changing it" (reverse-coded)
5. "After using AI, I feel like I understand the topic better"
6. "I use AI to learn how to do things I couldn't do before"

**Analytic payoff:**
- Create strategic vs. passive use composite
- Test: Do people who believed AI makes you smarter at W1 end up using it more strategically at W4?
- Link strategy use to self-reported learning/curiosity outcomes
- Feeds inequality story: education/wealth → sophistication of AI use

---

## Block 2: Construal Manipulation — Embedded Experiment (feeds Agency paper)

**Rationale:** The Agency paper (S3) hypothesizes that construing AI use at superordinate levels (outcome focus) vs. subordinate levels (process focus) increases overall perceived agency. The Gallup panel lets us test this at population scale with longitudinal baselines.

**Design:** Random assignment to one of three conditions (one paragraph each):

- **Outcome construal:** "Take a moment to think about the goals you've achieved with the help of AI tools — things you've accomplished, problems you've solved, or results you've produced."
- **Process construal:** "Take a moment to think about how you do your work when using AI tools — the steps you take, the decisions you make, and how involved you are in the process."
- **Control:** "Take a moment to think about your recent experiences using AI tools."

**Dependent measures (all conditions, 1-6 Likert):**
1. "I feel in control of what I accomplish when using AI" (outcome agency)
2. "AI helps me achieve my goals" (outcome agency)
3. "I feel in control of how my work gets done when using AI" (process agency)
4. "I feel personally involved in the process of my work when using AI" (process agency)
5. "Overall, using AI makes me feel more capable" (global agency)
6. "I feel satisfied with how I work with AI" (satisfaction)
7. "I worry about becoming too dependent on AI" (dependency concern)

**Unique value of doing this in Gallup:**
- We have W1-3 actual usage data, so we can control for real behavior when testing the construal effect
- We can test who responds to the manipulation: heavy delegators may benefit most from outcome construal
- The "ambition paradox" from W1 (Q8: ambitious people think AI makes *people* lazy but makes *them* less lazy) may BE spontaneous outcome construal — ambitious people naturally focus on what they accomplish. The manipulation gives everyone what ambitious people do automatically.
- Nationally representative sample makes this far more generalizable than a lab study

## Block 3: Polarization Items (feeds Twins/Synthetic Contact paper)

**Rationale:** The Twins paper shows 10-minute AI chatbot conversations with political outgroup increase warmth by 7.3 points. But we lack a "Study 1" showing that people *don't expect* this. Two items in Gallup give us the nationally representative lay belief as setup.

**Proposed items (pick 2):**

1. **Forecast item (highest priority):** "If someone spent 10 minutes talking to an AI chatbot representing the opposing political party, their feelings toward that party would become:" — much more negative / somewhat more negative / no change / somewhat more positive / much more positive

2. **Lay theory item:** "AI chatbots are making political divisions in this country worse" (1-6)

**Analytic payoff:**
- Compare population forecast against actual experimental result (7.3 point increase)
- If majority predicts "no change" or "more negative," we have the mismatch that frames the entire paper
- Link to partisan identity from earlier waves
- Can reference as "Study 1 (N=X, nationally representative)" in the Twins paper

**Note on demand effects:** We discussed whether the forecast item telegraphs our finding. Conclusion: no concern, because (a) Gallup respondents are not the experimental participants — different samples, no contamination, and (b) this is an established method (cf. DellaVigna & Pope forecasting paradigm).

## Optional Block 4: Parent Gatekeeping (extends Imas et al. + strengthens dyad story)

**Rationale:** W1 showed parental worry doesn't predict child usage. But we didn't measure what parents actually *do*. Imas et al. (NBER w34488, R&R at PNAS) showed parental demand for AI tools is driven by social norms, not beliefs about quality, and that risk information doesn't change demand — it just increases support for bans. We can extend this longitudinally.

**Proposed items (for parent respondents only, 1-6 Likert):**
1. "I set rules about when/how my child can use AI"
2. "I have talked with my child about responsible AI use"
3. "I check what my child uses AI for"
4. "I think schools should restrict AI use" (direct Imas comparison)
5. "Most of my child's friends use AI regularly" (social norm — Imas's key mechanism)
6. Parent open-ended: "What concerns you most about your child using AI?"

**Matched parent-child perception items (if room):**

| Parent version | Child version |
|---|---|
| "AI has made my child lazier" | "AI has made me lazier" |
| "AI has helped my child learn new things" | "AI has helped me learn new things" |
| "My child uses AI as a shortcut" | "I use AI as a shortcut" |

The perception gap between parent and child is a paper on its own.

**Include if there's room.** These are lower priority than Blocks 1-3 but very high upside if feasible.

## Summary: Priority Ranking

| Priority | Block | Items | Time | Feeds |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | AI Use Strategies | 6 items | ~2 min | Coach not Crutch |
| 2 | Construal Experiment | 1 paragraph + 7 items | ~3 min | Agency paper |
| 3 | Polarization | 2 items | ~30 sec | Twins paper |
| 4 | Parent Gatekeeping | 5-6 items + matched | ~2 min | Imas extension, dyad paper |

**Minimum viable version:** Block 1 + Block 3 (8 items, ~2.5 min). This advances the two strongest papers (Coach not Crutch, Twins) with minimal survey real estate.

**Full version:** All four blocks (~8-10 min added survey time). This feeds four papers and maximizes the value of the longitudinal panel.
